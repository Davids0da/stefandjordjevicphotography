The series was made when I was already living in Belgrade, during the weekends and summers I spent back in my hometown, Bor. In the beginning it was mainly nostalghia for family and friends. As the years gone by, and my visits became less frequent, I felt as if I didn’t belong there anymore. My childhood friends already had families and were employed in the mine - they were living the life their parents had lived. The city has not changed much since the generation shift, and the spirit of the people and attitudes towards life have remained the same to a great extent. As if the time had stopped.


"Kolos" is the name of my skatebording crew from my hometown, Bor, which was the biggest part of my adolescence. Growing up in a small industrial town doesn't offer much, and for all of usskateboarding was a sort of resistance against apathy and conformism. All of us were different,and we took different paths later in life, but what united us the most was an enormous desire to feel more. Skateboarding also evolved my passion for photography, the result of which can be seen throughout this series. We spent a lot of time together, and I enjoyed capturing this raw energy from the inside, for I feel all of them as my second family.


I first came to juvenile detention center "Kruška" in Kruševac for a documentary film I was working on as a director of photography. It was a small crew, along me there were the director Jovan Todorovic and sound recordist Aleksandar Perovic. Male and female residents of educational-correctional home are aged from 14 to 23 years old. Their time in the home varies from six months up to four years, because the time is not predetermined when the verdict is passed – they are on a constant psychological evaluation. The state grants them release only when it is certain that their moral values have taken the right turn, and they are prepared to be integrated back into society. Inside, they are divided among themselves in different groups: mangashi, fags, snitches... but there is also a number of those who try to keep to themselves, to serve their time, ang get out. One of the minors there was sentenced to time in Kruška for drunken driving, which led to an accident, and his brother’s death. Never before was he involved in anything on the other side of the law.
I liked going there, spending time with them, and at most times I felt uncomfortable telling them. Because I knew they wished they were anywhere but inside those walls.

My first visit to Paris was imagined as a way of getting acquainted with the French history and culture. Despite it being very impressive, my attention seemed to be drawn more to the people in my surroundings, especially during their “quiet times”. Tired of waiting in endless queues, overwhelmed by crowds and the enormous amount of information, people need a moment of peace and quiet, a moment to themselves. I was intrigued by that state of mind, when through all the noise you somehow fail to hear your own thoughts.





Stefan Djordjevic is an independent documentary photographer and director of photography, based in Belgrade, Serbia. He was born in Bor, a small industrial town in East Serbia in 1987. After moving to Belgrade for studying, his main interest switched from skateboarding to film and photography. He graduated cinematography at the Faculty of Dramatic Arts, and is currently working as a freelance director of photography on films, commercials and music videos.

His photography deals with human emotion and people’s relation to the space surrounding them, individually or within a group. No matter which media he is using, he is constantly exploring the personal impression of the contemporary socialissues and the stories hidden in the details.
